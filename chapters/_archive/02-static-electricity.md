## TL;DR

Electric charge is a conserved quantity that comes in two varieties — positive and negative — that repel their like and attract their opposite. The force between charges follows an inverse-square law: Coulomb's law, which is mathematically identical in form to gravity but dramatically stronger and capable of pushing as well as pulling. Electric fields and field lines are conceptual tools — a map of the forces a charge would feel if placed somewhere in space — and they work because they visualize what the mathematics predicts.

## Chapter Opening: The Photograph and the Physics

You have been given a photograph, or you've seen one: a child sliding down a playground slide, mouth open in that halfway point between laughter and shock. The next frame, if there were one, would show the small spark that jumps between the child's finger and another child's hand. A zap. A sting. An instant when the air itself becomes a conductor and electricity moves through it fast.

Static electricity. The word sounds like a contradiction — electricity that doesn't move. But the contradiction dissolves as soon as you look carefully at what is happening. The child has separated charge on her body from the metal slide. Electrons have been rubbed away. The slide now pulls on those missing electrons — on the absence where negative charge should be. The child has become a pocket of positive charge with no negative charges to balance it out. Someone else approaches with a normal, neutral body. The imbalance cannot hold. Electrons leap the gap. In that leap — in that instant of motion — the static breaks. But before the break, the imbalance was real. The forces were real. And they are what we need to understand.

This chapter is about those forces. Three things:

What is electric charge, and where does it come from? Why do charges repel their own kind and attract the opposite? And how do physicists visualize and calculate the forces that arise when charge is imbalanced in space?

We're going to answer each by unfolding a mechanism. The first is the mechanism by which matter becomes charged at all. The second is Coulomb's law — the equation that describes how strong the force between charges must be. The third is the idea of the electric field — not a mystical force field from science fiction, but a precise map that tells you, at any point in space, what force a test charge would experience.

## Concept 1: Electric Charge and Conservation

### The Discovery

You will have noticed, when you rubbed a comb through your hair, or rubbed a balloon on your shirt, that the comb or balloon then attracts small pieces of paper. The ancient Greeks knew this phenomenon. They noticed that amber, when rubbed by fur, attracts dry straw. The Greek word for amber is *elektron*. That is where the word *electric* comes from. For nearly 2,000 years, that is all anyone knew: rubbing things together made them do something to other things at a distance.

In the 1890s, the English physicist J. J. Thomson was studying rays of light coming from the negative terminal of a metal tube when electricity was passed through it. These "cathode rays," as they were called, deflected in the presence of a magnet. Thomson realized that the rays were not light at all. They were particles — very small particles, much smaller than atoms. In 1897, Thomson declared that he had discovered the particle carrying negative charge. We call it the electron today. Its charge is always the same: $-1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ coulombs.

Within a few years, Ernest Rutherford used a different approach. He fired tiny helium nuclei (which he called "alpha particles") at thin gold foil, expecting most to pass straight through if the atom was soft and mushy. Instead, some bounced straight back. When Rutherford heard the results, he said it was "as if you fired a shell at tissue paper and it came back and hit you." He realized that atoms were mostly empty space, with almost all the mass concentrated in a tiny, dense nucleus. Later experiments showed that this nucleus contained particles carrying positive charge. We call these particles protons. Each proton carries $+1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ coulombs — exactly opposite to the electron.

That is the machinery: electrons carry negative charge; protons carry positive charge. In a neutral atom, they are equal in number, so their charges cancel to zero. When you rub a comb through your hair, electrons are pulled off the atoms in your hair and transferred to the comb. The comb now has more electrons than protons — it has a net negative charge. Your hair has fewer electrons than it should — it has a net positive charge. Your hair stands on end because your hair and the comb now repel each other.

### The Rule: Conservation

There is one law that governs all of this: the law of conservation of charge. Charge can never be created or destroyed. It can be separated. It can be moved. It can be redistributed across many objects. But the total amount of positive and negative charge in any closed system is constant.

In numbers: suppose you have a system — say, a glass rod and a silk cloth — that together has zero net charge. You rub them together. An imbalance appears. The glass rod becomes positively charged by, let us say, $+2.0 \times 10^{-8}$ coulombs. The silk cloth must therefore become negatively charged by exactly $-2.0 \times 10^{-8}$ coulombs. Not $-2.1 \times 10^{-8}$. Not $-1.9 \times 10^{-8}$. Exactly opposite. The total charge in the system remains zero.

This is not a convenient rule. It is not an observation that usually holds. It is absolute. It has been tested in countless experiments, at energy scales from the subatomic to the cosmic, and it has never been violated. Charge is conserved.

What does this tell you about the rubbing? When you rub a comb through your hair, you are not creating charge. You are separating it. You are moving electrons from one object to another. The number of electrons that leave your hair equals the number that arrive at the comb. One object loses electrons (becomes positive); the other gains them (becomes negative). The charge that is lost by one is gained exactly by the other.

### Trade-off: How Charge Separates

There are three ways to separate charge in a material:

**Conduction** — direct contact. When a charged object touches an uncharged object, electrons can flow between them if the material allows it. Metals are *conductors*: electrons in metals are loosely bound to atoms and move freely. Rubber, glass, and plastic are *insulators*: electrons are tightly bound, and they do not move easily. This is why a comb rubbed on a rubber rod does not charge well: the electrons cannot flow between them. A comb rubbed on your hair, though — your hair is slightly conductive at the scales we're working with — the electrons transfer, and the comb becomes charged.

**Polarization** — shifting, not transferring. In an insulator like plastic, the electrons cannot leave their atoms. But when a charged object comes near, the electrons in the insulator are pulled slightly toward or pushed slightly away from the center of their atoms. The atom as a whole is still neutral, but the center of negative charge and the center of positive charge are no longer in the same place. The atom is polarized. A piece of plastic polarized in the presence of a charged comb will be attracted to the comb — the side closer to the comb becomes oppositely charged, attracting the comb. But once the comb is removed, the polarization disappears. There is no permanent charge transfer.

**Induction** — polarization followed by separation. Suppose you bring a charged rod near an uncharged metal sphere. The rod's charge polarizes the sphere — electrons crowd to one side. If, while the rod is still near, you ground the sphere by connecting it to a wire into the earth, the excess electrons escape into the ground. Now remove the ground wire. The rod is still near. But the electrons have gone. The side of the sphere nearest the rod is now positively charged. The opposite side — lacking electrons — is also positive. The sphere is now permanently charged, because the electrons that balanced it have been removed. This is induction.

### Worked Example: Counting Electrons

You have a dust particle that has acquired a charge of $-13$ nanocoulombs (where $1 \text{ nC} = 10^{-9} \text{ C}$). How many excess electrons does it carry?

The charge on one electron is $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C. Each excess electron contributes this amount of negative charge. The number of excess electrons is:

$$N = \frac{|-13 \times 10^{-9} \text{ C}|}{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C/electron}}$$

$$N = \frac{13 \times 10^{-9}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} = 8.1 \times 10^{10} \text{ electrons}$$

More than eighty billion excess electrons. Not visible to the eye. Not heavy. But enough to create forces that make the particle repel other particles of the same charge, or attract particles of opposite charge.

### Misconceptions

**"Static electricity is electricity that is not moving."** Partly true, partly misleading. The *charges* that create the static field are not moving continuously (they are not flowing as a current). But the process of separating them — of creating the imbalance in the first place — requires charges to move. A lightning strike is a huge current, yet it is created by static electricity. The word "static" refers to the condition of the charge imbalance, not to the motion required to create it.

**"You can add charge to a neutral object."** No. You separate charge. You move it from one place to another. The total charge in the system is unchanged. What changes is the distribution.

## Concept 2: Coulomb's Law — The Inverse-Square Force

### The Experiment

In the 1780s, the French physicist Charles-Augustin de Coulomb wanted to measure the force between charged objects. He built an instrument called a *torsion balance*: a rod suspended by a very thin wire, with a charged metal sphere at one end. He would charge another sphere and bring it near. The force between them would twist the wire. By turning a dial at the top, he could increase the twist until it balanced the electrical force. He could then measure how much twist was needed.

Here is what he discovered:

When he brought the second sphere **twice as close**, the force increased by a factor of **four**.

When he brought it **three times as close**, the force increased by a factor of **nine**.

When he brought it **four times as close**, the force increased by a factor of **sixteen**.

The pattern is exact: the force is proportional to the inverse of the square of the distance. If the distance is doubled, the force becomes one-quarter. If the distance is tripled, the force becomes one-ninth.

Coulomb also varied the charge. Double the charge on the first sphere, and the force doubles. Double the charge on both spheres, and the force quadruples. The force is proportional to the product of the charges.

### The Law

Combining these observations, Coulomb wrote:

$$F = \frac{kq_1q_2}{r^2}$$

where:
- $F$ is the magnitude of the force between the charges (in newtons)
- $q_1$ and $q_2$ are the charges (in coulombs)
- $r$ is the distance between their centers (in meters)
- $k$ is Coulomb's constant: $k = 8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N} \cdot \text{m}^2 / \text{C}^2$

This is the rule. It is inverse-square, which means the force depends on $1/r^2$. Another inverse-square law exists in physics — Newton's law of universal gravitation: $F_g = G m_1 m_2 / r^2$. The form is identical. The difference is in the constant. Gravity's constant is $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}$ in SI units. Coulomb's constant is $k = 8.99 \times 10^9$. That is a difference of $10^{20}$. The electrostatic force is twenty orders of magnitude stronger than gravity.

The direction of the force is along the line joining the two charges. If the charges have the same sign (both positive or both negative), the product $q_1 q_2$ is positive, so the force is repulsive — the charges push each other away. If the charges have opposite signs, the product is negative, and the force is attractive — the charges pull each other toward the center.

### Trade-off: How Small Is Small?

Coulomb's law works precisely when the charged objects can be treated as *point charges* — when they are much smaller than the distance between them, or when they are uniform spheres and the distance is measured from center to center.

For a dust particle and a droplet a few centimeters apart, this is fine. For a comb and your hair, the situation is more complex — the charges are distributed unevenly across the surface of each hair, so the calculation is approximate. The law still holds in its essential form: the force decreases as the inverse square of distance, and it is proportional to the product of charges. But the exact numerical answer requires knowing the precise distribution of charge on the comb and on each hair.

For most problems we encounter, we treat extended objects (rods, plates, spheres) as point charges concentrated at their center, and we accept the approximation it gives.

### Worked Example: The Force Between Two Charged Spheres

Coulomb measures a force of $20 \times 10^{-6}$ N between two charged spheres when they are separated by 5.0 cm. He then brings them to 3.0 cm apart. What force does he measure now?

Using Coulomb's law for the initial position:
$$F_i = \frac{kq_1q_2}{r_i^2}$$

Using Coulomb's law for the final position:
$$F_f = \frac{kq_1q_2}{r_f^2}$$

Dividing the second equation by the first:
$$\frac{F_f}{F_i} = \frac{r_i^2}{r_f^2}$$

So:
$$F_f = F_i \frac{r_i^2}{r_f^2} = (20 \times 10^{-6} \text{ N}) \frac{(5.0 \text{ cm})^2}{(3.0 \text{ cm})^2}$$

$$F_f = (20 \times 10^{-6} \text{ N}) \times \frac{25}{9} = (20 \times 10^{-6} \text{ N}) \times 2.78 = 5.6 \times 10^{-5} \text{ N}$$

The force increased by a factor of 2.78 when the distance decreased from 5.0 cm to 3.0 cm. This is less than the factor of 4 that would apply if the distance had halved exactly (from 5.0 to 2.5), but consistent with the $1/r^2$ rule.

### Misconceptions

**"Coulomb's law is just like gravity."** Yes, in form. But the implications are opposite. Gravity is always attractive — there is no such thing as negative mass, so all masses pull on each other. Charge is dualistic: like charges repel, opposite charges attract. This changes everything. Gravity builds structures that hold together; electrostatic repulsion tears them apart unless opposite charges are present to bind them.

**"The force between charges is constant once they are charged."** No. The force is a function of distance. If you pull two charged particles apart, the force between them weakens (proportionally to the inverse square of distance). If you push them together, the force strengthens. This is the mechanism of charging by induction — you move charges in and out of position, and the forces increase or decrease accordingly.

## Concept 3: Electric Fields and Field Lines

### The Idea

Suppose you have placed a positive charge $Q$ at the origin of a coordinate system. You want to know: if I place a test charge — a small positive charge $q$ — at some other location, what force will it experience?

You could use Coulomb's law every time: $F = k|Q||q| / r^2$. But there is a more elegant approach. You could define a property of space itself that surrounds the charge $Q$. That property would tell you, at any point in space, what force per unit charge any test charge would experience. We call this property the *electric field*.

The electric field is defined as:
$$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_{\text{test}}}$$

where $\vec{F}$ is the force on the test charge, and $q_{\text{test}}$ is the magnitude of the test charge. The units of the electric field are newtons per coulomb (N/C).

For a point charge $Q$ or a uniformly charged sphere, the magnitude of the electric field at distance $r$ is:
$$E = \frac{k|Q|}{r^2}$$

This is Coulomb's law rewritten. Instead of asking "what is the force on my test charge?" you ask "what is the electric field at this location?" If you then place a test charge $q$ in that field, the force on the charge is:
$$F = q \times E$$

This is not a shortcut, mathematically. It is the same calculation. But conceptually, it is a shift. It separates the question "what is the force from the charge $Q$?" from the question "what is my test charge?" You can map the field around $Q$ without reference to any test charge at all.

### Electric Field Lines

Here is how to visualize a field:

Imagine placing a small test charge at many points surrounding your source charge $Q$. At each point, measure the force. Draw an arrow at that point pointing in the direction the test charge would move, with the arrow's length proportional to the strength of the force (or the field). If you connect all these arrows, you get lines — lines that tell you the direction of the force at every point in space.

We call these *field lines*. Field lines from a positive charge point away from the charge — outward, in all directions. Field lines into a negative charge point toward the charge — inward, from all directions. If you have both charges present — a positive and a negative — the field lines flow out of the positive charge and into the negative charge.

Three rules for field lines:

1. **Field lines point in the direction a positive test charge would move.** A positive charge placed at a point on a field line would accelerate in the direction the line points.

2. **The density of field lines represents the strength of the field.** Where the lines are close together, the field is strong. Where they are far apart, the field is weak. This is not arbitrary — the number of lines is proportional to the charge creating the field, so the density is proportional to the field strength.

3. **Field lines cannot cross.** At any point in space, the electric field has one direction and one magnitude. Only one field line can pass through any point. If two field lines crossed, the field at that point would point in two directions at once, which is impossible.

### Trade-off: Visualization versus Precision

Field lines are not real — there is no actual line sitting in space pointing in a direction. Field lines are a tool, an abstraction. The electric field is the real thing: it is a mathematical entity that describes, at every point in space, the force per unit charge.

But field lines are powerful because they let you *see* what the mathematics predicts. A uniform field between two oppositely charged plates shows up as evenly spaced, parallel lines. A very strong field near a point charge shows up as dense lines clustering around the charge. A field created by two negative charges pushing each other apart shows lines that bow away from the region between them.

The trade-off is this: field lines give you the right direction at every point, and the right intuition about where the field is strong and where it is weak. They do not give you the precise numerical value of the field. For that, you use the equation $E = k|Q| / r^2$. But if you want to know which way a charged particle would move, or which configurations of charge would create a uniform field, or how fields interact when multiple charges are present, field lines are the faster path to understanding.

### Worked Example: Field Strength from a Doorknob

A metal doorknob acquires a static charge of $q = -1.5$ nC. The doorknob is roughly spherical with a diameter of 5.0 cm. What is the electric field at a point 1.0 cm in front of the doorknob's surface?

Since the doorknob is a conductor, the charge distributes uniformly on the outer surface. We can treat all the charge as if it were concentrated at the center of the sphere.

The radius of the doorknob is 2.5 cm = 0.025 m. The point in front of the surface is an additional 1.0 cm = 0.010 m away. So the distance from the center to our point is:
$$r = 0.025 \text{ m} + 0.010 \text{ m} = 0.035 \text{ m}$$

Using the equation for electric field from a point charge:
$$E = \frac{k|Q|}{r^2} = \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N} \cdot \text{m}^2 / \text{C}^2)(1.5 \times 10^{-9} \text{ C})}{(0.035 \text{ m})^2}$$

$$E = \frac{13.485 \text{ N} \cdot \text{m}^2 / \text{C}}{0.001225 \text{ m}^2} = 1.1 \times 10^4 \text{ N/C}$$

The field points toward the doorknob (since the charge is negative). This field is strong enough to move free electrons in the air — at about $3 \times 10^6$ N/C, air ionizes, and a spark can jump. Our doorknob's field, at about $1 \%$ of the ionization threshold, is not yet strong enough to break down air. But bring your finger very close, and the field becomes much stronger, and a spark jumps the gap.

### Misconceptions

**"Electric field lines are real."** They are not. They are a visualization tool. The real entity is the electric field — a property of space that exists at every point and describes the force per unit charge. The field lines we draw are one way to represent it. But the field exists even where we do not draw a line.

**"I can count the number of field lines to find the charge."** You can in principle — the number of lines is proportional to the charge — but only if you agree beforehand on how many lines to draw per coulomb of charge. This is not standardized. What is standardized is the density of lines: close packing means strong field; sparse packing means weak field.

## Integration: How These Three Concepts Connect

Electric charge is the fundamental property. It comes in two kinds, and it is conserved. When we separate charge — by friction, by conduction, by induction — we create an imbalance. Once separated, the charges interact by exerting forces on each other. Coulomb's law describes exactly how strong those forces are: inversely proportional to the square of distance, proportional to the product of the charges, attractive if opposite, repulsive if same.

But calculating forces between every pair of charges in a complex arrangement (like a charged comb and a head full of hair) would be tedious. So we use a shortcut: the electric field. We map the field created by one charge (or a distribution of charges). Then, for any test charge we place in that field, we can instantly calculate the force: $F = qE$. The field lines give us the direction; the field magnitude gives us the strength.

This is how the three concepts scaffold each other. Charge is what exists. Coulomb's law is how charges interact. The electric field is how we visualize and calculate those interactions efficiently.

### Why This Matters: A Practical Example

Consider the comb and your hair again. When you rub the comb through your hair, you separate electrons. The comb accumulates negative charge; your hair becomes positive. Now the charged comb approaches your hair. Using only conservation of charge, we know that repulsion will occur — opposite charges in the comb and hair attract, but like charges repel. Using Coulomb's law, we could calculate the force between the comb and one strand of hair, then sum over all the strands — a tedious calculation.

But using the electric field approach, we can ask: what is the field created by the charged comb at the location of a strand of hair? Then, knowing the charge on that hair strand, we calculate the force as $F = q_{\text{hair}} \times E_{\text{comb}}$. This is faster computationally, but more importantly, it reveals the structure of the problem: the field is independent of the hair strand's charge. The same field exists whether the hair is there or not. This perspective leads to deeper insights — for instance, we can now ask how superposition works: if two charges are present, we can add their fields, and the total field tells us the force on any third charge.

### Limitations and Boundaries

This framework is powerful but not unlimited. Coulomb's law assumes charges are stationary (not moving with respect to each other). When charges move, additional magnetic forces arise — that is the subject of electromagnetism, which comes later. The field picture still works, but we must account for the fact that a moving charge creates both an electric and a magnetic field.

At very small distances (below $10^{-15}$ m, the scale of atomic nuclei), quantum mechanics dominates. Coulomb's law still holds as an approximation, but quantum effects become significant. Charges are no longer particles in the classical sense; they are described by wavefunctions, and the field itself is quantized.

At extremely high energies, the distinction between the electromagnetic force and the weak nuclear force blurs. They unify into the electroweak force. This unification is part of modern physics, beyond the scope of this course.

Within the domain where Coulomb's law applies — macroscopic objects separated by distances from millimeters to kilometers — the framework of charge, Coulomb's law, and electric fields is complete and precise.

A positive charge of $+3.0 \times 10^{-9}$ C is located at the origin. A negative charge of $-1.0 \times 10^{-9}$ C is located at $x = 0.20$ m.

a) Calculate the electric field at $x = 0.10$ m (midway between the charges) due to each charge separately.

**Challenge: Field Line Interpretation**

You observe a pattern of electric field lines around an unknown arrangement of charges. The lines emerge from a certain region and converge on another region. Some lines are parallel and evenly spaced.

a) What can you infer about the sign of charge in the two regions?
b) In which region is the field stronger?
c) Why might the lines be parallel in some locations?

*Guidance:* Emergence suggests positive charge; convergence suggests negative charge. Density indicates strength. Parallel lines suggest a uniform field, which occurs between oppositely charged flat plates.

## Summary

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that comes in two varieties: positive and negative. Like charges repel; opposite charges attract. Charge is conserved — it cannot be created or destroyed, only separated and redistributed.

The force between charges is described by Coulomb's law: $F = kq_1q_2 / r^2$. This is an inverse-square law, identical in form to Newton's law of gravitation but twenty orders of magnitude stronger. The direction of the force is along the line joining the charges, with the force attractive for opposite charges and repulsive for like charges.

Electric fields and field lines provide a conceptual and computational tool. The electric field at any point in space is the force per unit charge that would be experienced by a test charge placed at that point. Field lines point in the direction of the field and have a density proportional to the field's strength. Together, charge, Coulomb's law, and field lines form a complete framework for understanding how charges interact across space without requiring physical contact.

## Connections Forward

This chapter has established static electricity — the separation of charge and the forces between separated charges. But separated charges cannot remain separated indefinitely if there is a path for them to recombine. In the next chapter, we will consider electric potential and potential energy — the work required to separate charges and the energy stored in that separation. From there, we will move to capacitors, which store charge (and energy) in engineered geometries, and finally to circuits, where charges move continuously through conductors, creating currents and magnetic effects.

Before those steps, though, notice what we have not yet explained: *why* electrons are attracted to protons. We have described it. We have measured it. We have written the law. But the *mechanism* — the *how* — remains mysterious. That is the nature of fundamental forces. Coulomb's law describes what happens when charges interact. Understanding *why* it works this way requires quantum field theory, which is far beyond this course. For now, accept that the universe contains two types of charge, and they interact according to this law. That is enough.
