# Chapter 10 — Electromagnetic Induction and Faraday's Law

*Change the flux and you get an EMF. The minus sign is energy conservation.*

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

1. **(Understand)** State **Faraday's law** $\varepsilon = -d\Phi_B/dt$ and identify the three independent ways the **magnetic flux** $\Phi_B = \iint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{A}$ through a loop can change.
2. **(Understand)** Explain why the minus sign — **Lenz's law** — is required by energy conservation, by reasoning through what would happen without it.
3. **(Apply)** Compute the **motional EMF** $\varepsilon = BLv$ for a rod sliding through a uniform field, both from Faraday's law and from the Lorentz force on the conduction electrons, and show the two derivations agree.
4. **(Apply)** Derive the AC generator output $\varepsilon(t) = NBA\omega \sin(\omega t)$ and compute peak and RMS values for realistic geometries.
5. **(Apply)** Use **mutual inductance** $M$ and the transformer ratio $V_2/V_1 = N_2/N_1$ to step voltage up or down at fixed power.
6. **(Apply)** Use **self-inductance** $L$ to compute the back-EMF $\varepsilon = -L\,dI/dt$, the solenoid value $L = \mu_0 n^2 V$, and the stored energy $U = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$.
7. **(Apply)** Solve the **RL circuit** for the exponential rise $I(t) = (\varepsilon/R)(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ with time constant $\tau = L/R$, and find the time to any given fraction of the final current.
8. **(Analyze)** Recognize **eddy currents** in a bulk conductor exposed to changing flux, and explain how lamination suppresses them in transformer cores.
9. **(Apply)** Build an interactive Faraday-induction simulator and verify the predicted dependence on magnet speed, coil turns, and coil area.

## Opening case: where the wall plug actually comes from

Roughly 85% of US electricity in 2024 was generated by spinning a coil inside a magnetic field. Coal, natural gas, nuclear, hydro, and wind plants differ in how they make the rotor turn — burn fuel to make steam, drop water through a turbine, let a wind blade catch the breeze — but the last step is identical. A multi-turn copper coil sits inside a strong steady magnetic field on a shaft. The shaft spins. The flux through the coil cycles. An EMF appears at the terminals, and that EMF, after a chain of transformers, ends up at your wall socket as 120 V, 60 Hz ([US Energy Information Administration, *Electric Power Monthly*, 2024](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/monthly/)). The remaining ~15% — mostly photovoltaic solar — is the only chunk of US generation that does not pass through a Faraday-law generator.

The TIKTOC for this book says "99%." It used to be true. Photovoltaic solar has grown enough that the safer number is closer to 85% in the US today and still north of 95% globally. The qualitative point survives: the modern electrical world is almost entirely Faraday's law, scaled.

The discovery itself was patient. On August 29, 1831, Michael Faraday wound two coils on opposite sides of a soft iron ring at the Royal Institution in London. One coil was connected through a switch to a battery; the other ran across the room to a galvanometer. He closed the switch. The galvanometer's needle deflected — *and then returned to zero* while the battery current kept flowing steadily through the first coil. When he opened the switch, the needle deflected the other way and again returned to zero ([Faraday, "Experimental Researches in Electricity. First Series," *Phil. Trans. R. Soc.* **122**, 125 (1832)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstl.1832.0006)). Joseph Henry, working alone at the Albany Academy, had seen essentially the same thing roughly a year earlier but didn't publish until 1832 ([Henry, *Am. J. Sci. Arts* **22**, 403 (1832)](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2495640)). The convention is to credit Faraday with the discovery and the law; Henry's name is on the SI unit of inductance.

The transient was the discovery. Steady current in coil A did nothing to coil B no matter how strong. Only *change* induced a current — the moment of switch-on and the moment of switch-off. James Clerk Maxwell, three decades later (1855–1865), wrote this transient down as the equation we now call Faraday's law. That equation is the subject of this chapter.

## Core concept

### Faraday's law: $\varepsilon = -d\Phi_B/dt$

For any closed loop bounded by a surface $S$, the **EMF** induced around the loop equals the negative time-derivative of the **magnetic flux** through $S$:

$$\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}, \qquad \Phi_B = \iint_S \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{A}.$$

For uniform $\vec{B}$ through a flat loop of area $A$ whose normal makes angle $\theta$ with $\vec{B}$, the flux collapses to $\Phi_B = BA\cos\theta$, and

$$\varepsilon = -\frac{d}{dt}(BA\cos\theta).$$

The unit of magnetic flux is the **weber** (Wb), with 1 Wb = 1 T·m². The unit of EMF is the volt, just like a battery, because functionally the induced EMF drives a current $\varepsilon/R$ through a resistive loop exactly as a battery would.

**Three independent ways the flux can change.** Each produces an EMF.
1. **$B$ changes in magnitude** at fixed geometry — a nearby AC source ramping up, a switching electromagnet, a moving permanent magnet whose field-strength at the loop is changing.
2. **$A$ changes** — a loop being stretched, compressed, or swept through the field. This is the rod-on-rails geometry that produces motional EMF.
3. **$\theta$ changes** — the loop is rotated in a fixed field. This is the generator geometry.

For a coil of $N$ turns wrapped on the same flux path, each turn contributes its own EMF in series, so

$$\varepsilon = -N\,\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}.$$

A quick note on language. In static circuits, *voltage* is the line integral of a conservative electric field between two points; it doesn't care which path you take. The induced electric field in Faraday's law is *not* conservative — $\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = -d\Phi_B/dt \neq 0$ around a loop enclosing changing flux. Path matters. **EMF** labels the line integral of this non-conservative field around a closed loop. Same units (volts), different conceptual object. After we have introduced the term, we will loosen back to "voltage" in worked examples; the distinction has done its job.

### Lenz's law: the minus sign is energy conservation

The minus sign in Faraday's law has a name. Heinrich Lenz, 1834: **the induced current flows in whichever direction creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in flux that produced it.**

Push the north pole of a bar magnet toward a coil. Flux through the coil is increasing in whichever direction the magnet's field points through the coil's plane. The induced current flows in the sense whose own magnetic moment $\vec{m} = IA\hat{n}$ points *back toward the approaching north* — the coil presents its own north pole to push back. You feel a force resisting the motion. You do mechanical work pushing through that resistance. That work becomes electrical energy in the coil, and then heat in the coil's resistance.

Pull the magnet out: flux is decreasing. Induced current reverses to maintain the flux it had. The coil now presents a south pole to the receding magnet, attracting it back. You again do work pulling it away.

**Energy conservation, as a one-line proof by contradiction.** Suppose the induced current did *not* oppose the change. Push a magnet at velocity $v$ toward the coil. The induced current then flows in the sense that *helps* the change — the coil's field attracts the incoming magnet. The magnet accelerates. As it accelerates, the rate of flux change increases, so the induced EMF increases, so the induced current increases, so the attractive force increases, so the magnet accelerates harder. Meanwhile the coil dissipates $I^2R$ in its resistance. You have a closed mechanical system whose kinetic energy is growing without bound while electrical energy is also being produced. Perpetual motion of the first kind, prohibited by the first law of thermodynamics. The only escape is the minus sign: induced currents oppose change, the magnet decelerates if you let it, and your hand pays the bill.

Lenz's law is not separate from Faraday's law. It *is* the physical content of the minus sign. Pedagogically convenient to teach them as two principles; conceptually a category error to count them as two laws.

### Motional EMF: $\varepsilon = BLv$

Specialize Faraday's law to a rigid conducting rod of length $L$ sliding at velocity $v$ along two stationary rails. A uniform $\vec{B}$ points perpendicular to both the rod and its motion (out of the page, say). The enclosed area grows at rate $dA/dt = Lv$, so the flux grows at $d\Phi_B/dt = BLv$, and

$$\varepsilon = BLv.$$

A second derivation arrives at the same answer by a different route. Each conduction electron inside the moving rod feels a Lorentz force $\vec{F} = q\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$ along the rod (Chapter 8). This force separates charge until an electrostatic field inside the rod balances the magnetic force on now-stationary electrons. The accumulated potential difference end-to-end of the rod is exactly $BLv$. The rod behaves like a battery with EMF $BLv$ and an internal resistance equal to its own resistance.

If the rails close the circuit through external resistance $R$, the current is $I = BLv/R$. The current-carrying rod inside $\vec{B}$ then feels a force $F = BIL = B^2L^2v/R$ *opposing its motion* (Lenz again — the force is the macroscopic shadow of the minus sign). To keep the rod moving at $v$, an external agent must supply mechanical power

$$P_{\text{mech}} = Fv = \frac{B^2L^2v^2}{R}.$$

The electrical power dissipated in $R$ is

$$P_{\text{elec}} = I^2R = \frac{(BLv)^2}{R}.$$

They are identical. Mechanical work in equals electrical energy out, with the field-times-current force as the bookkeeping device. The two derivations agree by no accident — this is one of the famous symmetries of Maxwell theory, and a foretaste of the relativistic unification of $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ that closes the book.

### The AC generator: $\varepsilon(t) = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)$

A flat coil of $N$ turns and area $A$ rotates at angular frequency $\omega$ inside a uniform $\vec{B}$ perpendicular to the rotation axis. At time $t$ the coil's normal makes angle $\omega t$ with $\vec{B}$, so

$$\Phi_B(t) = NBA\cos(\omega t),$$

and

$$\varepsilon(t) = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t).$$

The peak EMF is $\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega$. The RMS value used in AC power analysis is $\varepsilon_{\text{rms}} = \varepsilon_0/\sqrt{2}$.

The line frequency is a mechanical choice. A 60 Hz generator runs at $\omega = 2\pi(60) \approx 377$ rad/s. With a two-pole rotor on a steam turbine, that's 3600 rpm — a convenient steam-turbine shaft speed. 50 Hz (most of Europe, Africa, much of Asia) corresponds to 3000 rpm. The frequency was effectively set by available turbine speeds in the early 20th century and has been locked in by installed base since.

### Mutual inductance and the transformer

Two coils share flux through a common geometry (often an iron core that channels the flux). A current $I_1$ in coil 1 produces a flux $\Phi_{21}$ through each turn of coil 2. Define **mutual inductance** $M$ by

$$N_2 \Phi_{21} = M I_1.$$

It is a theorem of magnetostatics (not just a convention) that the same $M$ describes the reverse coupling: $N_1\Phi_{12} = M I_2$. Mutual inductance is symmetric.

Faraday's law then gives the induced EMF in coil 2 from coil 1's changing current:

$$\varepsilon_2 = -M\,\frac{dI_1}{dt}.$$

**The ideal transformer.** Both primary and secondary wound on a common iron core, so that essentially all flux from one passes through the other. The same $d\Phi/dt$ threads each turn on each side, so the EMF *per turn* is the same on both sides:

$$\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}.$$

Power conservation in an ideal transformer means $V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$, so

$$\frac{I_2}{I_1} = \frac{N_1}{N_2}.$$

Step *up* the voltage, step *down* the current by the same factor. Power is conserved (the transformer does *not* amplify power; it trades voltage for current). Real transformers lose 1–3% to copper resistance in the windings, eddy-current losses in the core, and hysteresis cycling of the iron. Big utility transformers achieve 98–99.5% efficiency. The ideal-transformer formula is what the real device delivers when those losses are small.

This is the technology that makes long-distance power transmission economic. Line losses go as $I^2R$. Doubling $V$ at the same delivered power halves $I$ and quarters the loss. At 345 kV (a standard US transmission voltage) vs. 240 V (residential), the same power through the same wire dissipates a factor $(345{,}000/240)^2 \approx 2\times 10^6$ less. That's why every long-haul transmission line in the world runs at 100s of kV.

### Self-inductance: $\varepsilon = -L\,dI/dt$

A current $I$ in a coil produces a magnetic flux through itself. In linear magnetics (no core saturation), the flux is proportional to $I$. Define **self-inductance** $L$ by

$$N\Phi = LI, \qquad L = \frac{N\Phi}{I}.$$

Units: the **henry** (H) = Wb/A = V·s/A. Named for Joseph Henry.

If $I$ changes in time, its own flux changes, and Faraday's law gives an induced EMF that *opposes* the change:

$$\varepsilon = -L\,\frac{dI}{dt}.$$

This is Lenz again: the coil fights changes in its own current. It does so by storing energy in its magnetic field during current rise and giving that energy back during current fall.

**Solenoid inductance.** For a long solenoid of $n$ turns per unit length, cross-sectional area $A$, length $\ell$ (so $N = n\ell$): inside the solenoid $B = \mu_0 n I$ (Chapter 9). Flux per turn $\Phi = BA = \mu_0 n I A$. Total flux linkage $N\Phi = n\ell \cdot \mu_0 n I A = \mu_0 n^2 V \cdot I$, where $V = \ell A$ is the solenoid's interior volume. Therefore

$$\boxed{\;L_{\text{solenoid}} = \mu_0 n^2 V.\;}$$

Sanity check: a 1000-turn solenoid 10 cm long with 1 cm² cross-section has $n = 10^4$ turns/m, $V = 10^{-5}$ m³, $L = (4\pi\times 10^{-7})(10^8)(10^{-5}) \approx 1.3$ mH. Iron-cored inductors multiply this by the relative permeability $\mu_r$ (hundreds to thousands), reaching the henry range.

### Energy stored in an inductor: $U = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$

Build current $I$ in an inductor from zero. The back-EMF $\varepsilon = -L\,dI/dt$ opposes the rise. The external source must supply power $P = LI\,(dI/dt)$ against this back-EMF. Integrate from 0 to $I$:

$$U = \int_0^I L I'\, dI' = \tfrac{1}{2}L I^2.$$

For a long solenoid with $L = \mu_0 n^2 V$ and $B = \mu_0 n I$:

$$U = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu_0 n^2 V \cdot I^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{B^2}{\mu_0}\cdot V = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}\cdot V.$$

The **magnetic energy density** is therefore

$$\boxed{\;u_B = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}.\;}$$

This is the magnetic analogue of $u_E = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$ from Chapter 5. The deep claim — and it is a deep one — is that the energy lives *in the field*, not in the wires that made the field. A superconducting loop disconnected from its power supply but with persistent current still flowing has energy $\tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$ stored in the volume the field occupies. Cut the wire and the field collapses; the energy emerges as a spark, an EMF spike, infrared heat. This claim is the bridge from Chapter 5's electric-field energy to Chapter 11's energy of a propagating EM wave.

### RL circuits: $\tau = L/R$

Series circuit: an ideal battery $\varepsilon$, resistor $R$, inductor $L$, and switch. Close the switch at $t = 0$. Kirchhoff's voltage law:

$$\varepsilon - IR - L\,\frac{dI}{dt} = 0.$$

Standard linear ODE; solution:

$$\boxed{\;I(t) = \frac{\varepsilon}{R}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau = \frac{L}{R}.\;}$$

The current rises exponentially toward the final value $\varepsilon/R$ with **time constant** $\tau$. The inductor's back-EMF starts at $+\varepsilon$ (perfectly opposing the source, current zero) and decays to zero (no flux change at steady state, current at $\varepsilon/R$). Open the source and short the inductor through $R$ and the solution mirrors: $I(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$.

The RL circuit is the magnetic mirror of the RC circuit from Chapter 5:

| Quantity | RC | RL |
|---|---|---|
| Time constant | $\tau = RC$ | $\tau = L/R$ |
| Stored energy | $\tfrac{1}{2}CV^2$ | $\tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$ |
| Stored in | Electric field | Magnetic field |
| Energy density | $\tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$ | $B^2/(2\mu_0)$ |
| Element resists changes in | Voltage | Current |

Notice the *inverted* dependence on $R$: a bigger $R$ makes RC *slower* but RL *faster*. That inversion catches students. Derive the time constant from the ODE rather than borrowing the RC formula.

### Eddy currents

Faraday's law applies to *any* closed conducting path, including paths you didn't intend. A solid metal plate inside a region of changing $\vec{B}$ (or moving through a non-uniform stationary $\vec{B}$) develops induced loop currents in its bulk — **eddy currents**. By Lenz, those currents produce fields that oppose the change, so the plate feels a retarding force whenever the flux through it is changing.

Three engineering consequences:

1. **Magnetic braking.** Drop a strong neodymium magnet down a copper or aluminum tube; it falls slowly because eddy currents in the wall produce a retarding force that nearly balances gravity. Used in regenerative-braking systems for trains, roller coasters, and some elevators.
2. **Transformer core losses.** A solid iron transformer core would itself act like a fat conducting loop and dissipate huge eddy-current losses. The fix is **lamination** — building the core from thin ($\sim 0.3$ mm) varnished sheets, each insulated from the next, so eddy loops can't close through the bulk.
3. **Induction cooktops.** Drive a flat spiral coil at ~25 kHz beneath a glass surface. The AC field induces eddy currents in ferromagnetic cookware sitting on top; the pan heats from $I^2R$ losses in its own bulk while the cooktop glass stays cool. Aluminum and copper pans barely heat — low $\mu_r$ and large skin depth mean poor coupling. The pan has to be ferrous ([Lucia et al., *IEEE Trans. Industrial Electronics* **61**, 2509 (2014)](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/6661334)).

Wireless phone chargers are the same mechanism at smaller scale. The Qi standard ([Wireless Power Consortium, 2008–](https://www.wirelesspowerconsortium.com/)) drives ~110–205 kHz between a transmit coil in the pad and a receive coil in the phone. Coupling is weak (the two coils are not on a shared iron core; they are loosely coupled inductors with mutual inductance $M$), and efficiency is alignment-dependent — 70–85% with good alignment, dropping fast with offsets of a centimeter. Apple's MagSafe (2020) added a ring of permanent magnets around the coil specifically to enforce alignment; Qi2 (December 2023) standardized that mechanism.

### A flag we won't fix in this chapter

Faraday's law as written above presupposes a well-defined material circuit. There are subtle cases — Faraday's own 1831 homopolar disk, sliding contacts, conducting bulk in motion — where the flux rule $\varepsilon = -d\Phi_B/dt$ and the Lorentz-force calculation $\oint(\vec{v}\times\vec{B})\cdot d\vec{\ell}$ both still work, but you have to be careful about which loop you mean ([Feynman, *Lectures on Physics* Vol. II, §17-2](https://www.feynmanlectures.caltech.edu/II_17.html)). For intro purposes this is a footnote, not a derailment. The flux rule is correct on every well-defined loop in this chapter.

## Worked example: trace the generator end-to-end

A flat coil of $N = 100$ turns, area $A = 0.01$ m², rotates at $\omega = 60\pi$ rad/s inside a uniform field $B = 0.5$ T perpendicular to the rotation axis. Find the peak EMF, the RMS EMF, and the output frequency.

**Setup.** The coil's normal makes angle $\omega t$ with $\vec{B}$, so

$$\Phi_B(t) = NBA\cos(\omega t).$$

(Including $N$ here is a notational shortcut for the total flux linkage; each turn sees flux $BA\cos\omega t$ and there are $N$ turns wired in series.)

**The EMF.** Take the time derivative:

$$\varepsilon(t) = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t).$$

**Peak.** The peak occurs when $\sin(\omega t) = 1$:

$$\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega = (100)(0.5)(0.01)(60\pi) = 30\pi \approx 94 \text{ V}.$$

**RMS.** For a sinusoid, $\varepsilon_{\text{rms}} = \varepsilon_0/\sqrt{2} \approx 67$ V.

**Frequency.** $f = \omega/(2\pi) = (60\pi)/(2\pi) = 30$ Hz. (Note that this is *not* US line frequency; a 60 Hz generator runs at $\omega = 120\pi$. The TIKTOC value $\omega = 60\pi$ produces a 30 Hz output. For 60 Hz line frequency you'd double $\omega$ and double the peak EMF to $\sim 188$ V.)

**The lesson.** Faraday's law turns mechanical work (the shaft torque keeping the coil spinning against the magnetic restoring torque from its own induced current) into electrical energy (the EMF available at the brushes). The conversion goes through the changing flux. No flux change, no EMF, no electricity. The minus sign in Faraday's law *is* the conservation of energy: every joule of electrical output traces back to a joule of mechanical work done on the shaft. Without the minus sign you have a perpetual-motion machine — turn the shaft and it accelerates itself faster.

**The limit.** Real generators are not ideal. Iron-core losses (eddy currents in the rotor and stator iron), copper resistance in the windings, and hysteresis losses cycling the iron each cost a percent or two. Large utility-scale generators reach 95–99% efficiency; small portable generators much less. The ideal-coil formula above is what the real machine delivers when those losses are small. The same warning applies to the transformer: the formula $V_2/V_1 = N_2/N_1$ is the ideal limit; the real device achieves 98–99.5% efficiency in big utility-scale units and somewhat less in small ones.

## Common misconceptions

**"Faraday's law requires a wire loop."** The wire is not the source of the effect; the wire is one path along which the induced EMF drives a current. Faraday's law (in its full Maxwell form, Chapter 11) says: a changing $\vec{B}$ creates a non-conservative $\vec{E}$ at every point in space, whether or not a wire is there. Drop the wire and the induced $\vec{E}$ is still there; it just has nothing to push on. The wire makes the effect *measurable* by giving the field somewhere to do work.

**"Lenz's law is a separate law from Faraday's."** Lenz's law *is* the minus sign in Faraday's law. The minus sign is mandated by energy conservation, as the one-line proof by contradiction above showed. Teaching them as two laws is pedagogically convenient and conceptually wrong. They are one statement read two ways: Faraday gives you the magnitude, Lenz reads you the sign.

**"Transformers amplify power."** They do not. They trade voltage for current at fixed power. A 100:1 step-up transformer multiplies the input voltage by 100 and divides the input current by 100. The output power $V_2 I_2 = V_1 I_1$ (minus a percent or two of losses). The power on the secondary side has to come from somewhere, and the only "somewhere" is the primary side. Nothing in the magnetic coupling between the two coils adds energy.

**"Induction needs motion."** It does not. Faraday's iron-ring experiment (1831) had no moving parts — only a switch being thrown. A stationary coil sitting next to a wire carrying ramping AC has an induced EMF from $\partial B/\partial t$ alone. A stationary loop inside an MRI scanner during a gradient switch picks up an induced EMF from $\partial B/\partial t \sim 50$ T/s; in tissue this can produce nerve stimulation if the rate is high enough ([Schaefer, Bourland, & Nyenhuis, *J. Magn. Reson. Imaging* **12**, 20 (2000)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/1522-2586%28200007%2912%3A1%3C20%3A%3AAID-JMRI3%3E3.0.CO%3B2-Y)).

**"In an RL circuit, bigger R is slower."** That's RC intuition. RL is the other way: $\tau = L/R$, so a bigger $R$ makes the rise *faster*. Derive the time constant from the ODE rather than recycling the RC formula.

- Set $N = 100$, $A = 0.01$ m², $R = 10$ Ω. Move the magnet toward the coil at constant speed. Read off the peak induced current; confirm it scales linearly when you double the speed.
- Move the magnet *through* the coil (toward, then away). Confirm the induced current reverses sign at the moment the magnet is closest to the coil's center — that's when $d\Phi_B/dt$ changes sign.
- Increase $R$. Does the EMF change? Does the current? (EMF should be unaffected — it depends only on $d\Phi_B/dt$. Current scales as $1/R$.)
- Set the coil $N = 500$ turns and slowly bring the magnet up. Read the EMF at small displacements. Compare to the analytic prediction $\varepsilon = -N\,dB/dt \cdot A$ evaluated at the magnet–coil separation.

### Extension prompt (chapter bridge to Chapter 11)

> **Show.** Faraday's law says: a changing magnetic flux drives a circulation of $\vec{E}$. Schematically, **changing $\vec{B}$ creates $\vec{E}$**. Maxwell stared at the equations of electromagnetism in 1861 and asked the obvious symmetric question: does a changing $\vec{E}$ create $\vec{B}$? The un-augmented Ampère's law from Chapter 9 said no — only conduction currents create $\vec{B}$. Maxwell noticed this answer was inconsistent with charge conservation at a charging capacitor.
>
> **Say.** Modify the simulator to also show, in a side panel, the electric flux $\Phi_E$ through a horizontal disk between two capacitor plates that are being charged. Show $d\Phi_E/dt$ as a third time-series.

Compute $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$ between the plates (treat plates as infinite, ignore fringing). Compute $\Phi_E = EA_{\text{cap}}$ through a disk between the plates. Faraday says changing $\vec{B}$ makes $\vec{E}$. Maxwell adds: changing $\vec{E}$ makes $\vec{B}$. Together, they propagate.

## What would change my mind

Faraday's law in its integral form $\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{\ell} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ is exact at every scale where classical electromagnetism applies, and has been confirmed in every transformer, generator, MRI scanner, and wireless charger ever built. A reproducible discrepancy between the measured EMF on a well-defined loop and the predicted $-d\Phi_B/dt$ would force a rewrite. None has been observed.

The minus sign — Lenz's law — is enforced by the first law of thermodynamics. A working device that gave currents in the *wrong* direction (a coil that accelerated an incoming magnet rather than repelling it) would simultaneously violate energy conservation and Maxwell's equations. Both would have to break. No such device exists.

The "85% of US electricity is Faraday induction" figure is empirical and will shift as the generation mix changes. If photovoltaic solar and battery output grow to (say) 50% of US generation by 2040, the Faraday-induction share will drop correspondingly. The *law* doesn't change; the share of the grid that exercises it does.

## Still puzzling

- *Why are Faraday's law and the Lorentz-force calculation equivalent for motional EMF?* In the lab frame the flux changes because the loop sweeps through $\vec{B}$. In the rod's rest frame there is no motional EMF — only an induced $\vec{E}$ from the field that, in the new frame, looks partly electric. Both calculations give the same answer. This is not a coincidence; it is a consequence of $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ being two components of a single field tensor under Lorentz transformation. The book returns to this in the capstone.
- *Iron-core nonlinearities.* Real transformer and inductor cores saturate at high $B$, exhibit hysteresis loops, and lose energy to eddy currents and to the cycling of domain walls. The clean formula $L = \mu_0 n^2 V$ assumes linear, lossless magnetics. Engineering practice deals with the corrections empirically (B–H curves, loss tables); a deeper theoretical treatment lives in solid-state physics.
- *The homopolar disk.* Faraday's own 1831 disk generator produces an EMF even though the obvious circuit loop you might draw shows no flux change. The resolution requires going back to the Lorentz-force-on-carriers picture, because the conduction path is through rotating bulk rather than a rigid wire loop. The flux rule and the force calculation are equivalent on well-defined loops; on subtler geometries you have to be careful about which loop you mean.
